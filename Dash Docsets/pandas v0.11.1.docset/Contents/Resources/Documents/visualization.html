Plotting with matplotlib¶

Note

We intend to build more plotting integration with matplotlib as time goes on.

We use the standard convention for referencing the matplotlib API:

In [1859]: import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

Basic plotting: plot¶

See the cookbook for some advanced strategies

The plot method on Series and DataFrame is just a simple wrapper around plt.plot:

In [1860]: ts = Series(randn(1000), index=date_range('1/1/2000', periods=1000))

In [1861]: ts = ts.cumsum()

In [1862]: ts.plot()
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x114488d50>

If the index consists of dates, it calls gcf().autofmt_xdate() to try to format the x-axis nicely as per above. The method takes a number of arguments for controlling the look of the plot:

In [1863]: plt.figure(); ts.plot(style='k--', label='Series'); plt.legend()
<matplotlib.legend.Legend at 0x114a2d250>

On DataFrame, plot is a convenience to plot all of the columns with labels:

In [1864]: df = DataFrame(randn(1000, 4), index=ts.index, columns=list('ABCD'))

In [1865]: df = df.cumsum()

In [1866]: plt.figure(); df.plot(); plt.legend(loc='best')
<matplotlib.legend.Legend at 0x10feacc10>

You may set the legend argument to False to hide the legend, which is shown by default.

In [1867]: df.plot(legend=False)
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x114483f50>

Some other options are available, like plotting each Series on a different axis:

In [1868]: df.plot(subplots=True, figsize=(6, 6)); plt.legend(loc='best')
<matplotlib.legend.Legend at 0x11460f910>

You may pass logy to get a log-scale Y axis.

In [1869]: plt.figure();
In [1869]: ts = Series(randn(1000), index=date_range('1/1/2000', periods=1000))

In [1870]: ts = np.exp(ts.cumsum())

In [1871]: ts.plot(logy=True)
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x11460f510>

You can plot one column versus another using the x and y keywords in DataFrame.plot:

In [1872]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x114e8c410>

In [1873]: df3 = DataFrame(randn(1000, 2), columns=['B', 'C']).cumsum()

In [1874]: df3['A'] = Series(range(len(df)))

In [1875]: df3.plot(x='A', y='B')
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x1066c6dd0>

Plotting on a Secondary Y-axis¶

To plot data on a secondary y-axis, use the secondary_y keyword:

In [1876]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x114f2ad90>

In [1877]: df.A.plot()
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x114f29a50>

In [1878]: df.B.plot(secondary_y=True, style='g')
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x114f23210>

Selective Plotting on Secondary Y-axis¶

To plot some columns in a DataFrame, give the column names to the secondary_y keyword:

In [1879]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x11405b4d0>

In [1880]: ax = df.plot(secondary_y=['A', 'B'])

In [1881]: ax.set_ylabel('CD scale')
<matplotlib.text.Text at 0x115350490>

In [1882]: ax.right_ax.set_ylabel('AB scale')
<matplotlib.text.Text at 0x115355990>

Note that the columns plotted on the secondary y-axis is automatically marked with “(right)” in the legend. To turn off the automatic marking, use the mark_right=False keyword:

In [1883]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x114fe72d0>

In [1884]: df.plot(secondary_y=['A', 'B'], mark_right=False)
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x11500dd90>

Suppressing tick resolution adjustment¶

Pandas includes automatically tick resolution adjustment for regular frequency time-series data. For limited cases where pandas cannot infer the frequency information (e.g., in an externally created twinx), you can choose to suppress this behavior for alignment purposes.

Here is the default behavior, notice how the x-axis tick labelling is performed:

In [1885]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x114fe7550>

In [1886]: df.A.plot()
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x114f7e610>

Using the x_compat parameter, you can suppress this behavior:

In [1887]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x114f69150>

In [1888]: df.A.plot(x_compat=True)
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x114fbae90>

If you have more than one plot that needs to be suppressed, the use method in pandas.plot_params can be used in a with statement:

In [1889]: import pandas as pd

In [1890]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x114f92950>

In [1891]: with pd.plot_params.use('x_compat', True):
   ......:     df.A.plot(color='r')
   ......:     df.B.plot(color='g')
   ......:     df.C.plot(color='b')
   ......:

Targeting different subplots¶

You can pass an ax argument to Series.plot to plot on a particular axis:

In [1892]: fig, axes = plt.subplots(nrows=2, ncols=2)

In [1893]: df['A'].plot(ax=axes[0,0]); axes[0,0].set_title('A')
<matplotlib.text.Text at 0x1153b3690>

In [1894]: df['B'].plot(ax=axes[0,1]); axes[0,1].set_title('B')
<matplotlib.text.Text at 0x1153d1b90>

In [1895]: df['C'].plot(ax=axes[1,0]); axes[1,0].set_title('C')
<matplotlib.text.Text at 0x1153e8d50>

In [1896]: df['D'].plot(ax=axes[1,1]); axes[1,1].set_title('D')
<matplotlib.text.Text at 0x117aa3550>

Other plotting features¶

Bar plots¶

For labeled, non-time series data, you may wish to produce a bar plot:

In [1897]: plt.figure();
In [1897]: df.ix[5].plot(kind='bar'); plt.axhline(0, color='k')
<matplotlib.lines.Line2D at 0x114f37810>

Calling a DataFrame’s plot method with kind='bar' produces a multiple bar plot:

In [1898]: df2 = DataFrame(rand(10, 4), columns=['a', 'b', 'c', 'd'])

In [1899]: df2.plot(kind='bar');

To produce a stacked bar plot, pass stacked=True:

In [1899]: df2.plot(kind='bar', stacked=True);

To get horizontal bar plots, pass kind='barh':

In [1899]: df2.plot(kind='barh', stacked=True);

Box-Plotting¶

DataFrame has a boxplot method which allows you to visualize the distribution of values within each column.

For instance, here is a boxplot representing five trials of 10 observations of a uniform random variable on [0,1).

In [1904]: df = DataFrame(rand(10,5))

In [1905]: plt.figure();
In [1905]: bp = df.boxplot()

You can create a stratified boxplot using the by keyword argument to create groupings. For instance,

In [1906]: df = DataFrame(rand(10,2), columns=['Col1', 'Col2'] )

In [1907]: df['X'] = Series(['A','A','A','A','A','B','B','B','B','B'])

In [1908]: plt.figure();
In [1908]: bp = df.boxplot(by='X')

You can also pass a subset of columns to plot, as well as group by multiple columns:

In [1909]: df = DataFrame(rand(10,3), columns=['Col1', 'Col2', 'Col3'])

In [1910]: df['X'] = Series(['A','A','A','A','A','B','B','B','B','B'])

In [1911]: df['Y'] = Series(['A','B','A','B','A','B','A','B','A','B'])

In [1912]: plt.figure();
In [1912]: bp = df.boxplot(column=['Col1','Col2'], by=['X','Y'])

Andrews Curves¶

Andrews curves allow one to plot multivariate data as a large number of curves that are created using the attributes of samples as coefficients for Fourier series. By coloring these curves differently for each class it is possible to visualize data clustering. Curves belonging to samples of the same class will usually be closer together and form larger structures.

Note: The “Iris” dataset is available here.

In [1918]: from pandas import read_csv

In [1919]: from pandas.tools.plotting import andrews_curves

In [1920]: data = read_csv('data/iris.data')

In [1921]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x11b301250>

In [1922]: andrews_curves(data, 'Name')
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x11bceacd0>

Parallel Coordinates¶

Parallel coordinates is a plotting technique for plotting multivariate data. It allows one to see clusters in data and to estimate other statistics visually. Using parallel coordinates points are represented as connected line segments. Each vertical line represents one attribute. One set of connected line segments represents one data point. Points that tend to cluster will appear closer together.

In [1923]: from pandas import read_csv

In [1924]: from pandas.tools.plotting import parallel_coordinates

In [1925]: data = read_csv('data/iris.data')

In [1926]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x11cf22490>

In [1927]: parallel_coordinates(data, 'Name')
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x11cf33b90>

Lag Plot¶

Lag plots are used to check if a data set or time series is random. Random data should not exhibit any structure in the lag plot. Non-random structure implies that the underlying data are not random.

In [1928]: from pandas.tools.plotting import lag_plot

In [1929]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x11cf4bd50>

In [1930]: data = Series(0.1 * rand(1000) +
   ......:    0.9 * np.sin(np.linspace(-99 * np.pi, 99 * np.pi, num=1000)))
   ......: 

In [1931]: lag_plot(data)
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x11cf903d0>

Autocorrelation Plot¶

Autocorrelation plots are often used for checking randomness in time series. This is done by computing autocorrelations for data values at varying time lags. If time series is random, such autocorrelations should be near zero for any and all time-lag separations. If time series is non-random then one or more of the autocorrelations will be significantly non-zero. The horizontal lines displayed in the plot correspond to 95% and 99% confidence bands. The dashed line is 99% confidence band.

In [1932]: from pandas.tools.plotting import autocorrelation_plot

In [1933]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x11dab6f10>

In [1934]: data = Series(0.7 * rand(1000) +
   ......:    0.3 * np.sin(np.linspace(-9 * np.pi, 9 * np.pi, num=1000)))
   ......: 

In [1935]: autocorrelation_plot(data)
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x11dabc450>

Bootstrap Plot¶

Bootstrap plots are used to visually assess the uncertainty of a statistic, such as mean, median, midrange, etc. A random subset of a specified size is selected from a data set, the statistic in question is computed for this subset and the process is repeated a specified number of times. Resulting plots and histograms are what constitutes the bootstrap plot.

In [1936]: from pandas.tools.plotting import bootstrap_plot

In [1937]: data = Series(rand(1000))

In [1938]: bootstrap_plot(data, size=50, samples=500, color='grey')
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x11dae8ad0>

RadViz¶

RadViz is a way of visualizing multi-variate data. It is based on a simple spring tension minimization algorithm. Basically you set up a bunch of points in a plane. In our case they are equally spaced on a unit circle. Each point represents a single attribute. You then pretend that each sample in the data set is attached to each of these points by a spring, the stiffness of which is proportional to the numerical value of that attribute (they are normalized to unit interval). The point in the plane, where our sample settles to (where the forces acting on our sample are at an equilibrium) is where a dot representing our sample will be drawn. Depending on which class that sample belongs it will be colored differently.

Note: The “Iris” dataset is available here.

In [1939]: from pandas import read_csv

In [1940]: from pandas.tools.plotting import radviz

In [1941]: data = read_csv('data/iris.data')

In [1942]: plt.figure()
<matplotlib.figure.Figure at 0x11dae8b90>

In [1943]: radviz(data, 'Name')
<matplotlib.axes.AxesSubplot at 0x11e3bb7d0>
